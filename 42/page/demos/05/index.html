January 9, 2019

Dear Grama,

I heard from dad you just had surgery. I hope your recovery is going well. I truly admire your strength and will to live. I think I inherited that from you. I hope to live to be 100. That might be because I feel my life is just beginning. I endured afflictions for a reason. They have certainly changed who I am all the way to the core. So much so, I wonder how to have a conversation with someone who knew the old me.

I wanted to write to you sooner. What empowered me to finally do so is that I made new habits. I have since signed off of text message and email. The purpose text messaging serves is not the kind of life I want to live. Technology makes it easy to connect but lacks content worthy of its alarming interruptions. My choices are enabling me to have moments like this one in which I write you a letter; Quiet. Focused. Meaningful. I am satisfied with how I spend my time when it is activities like these.

Thankfully, my vocation aligns with my aspirations. As a Computer Programmer, my job requires concentration on complex subjects which means I have to turn off alarms during work hours. I see no incentive to turn them back on.

When I’m not working, I spend the majority of time reading, writing, and thinking. I have much to reconsider. The circumstances of the past five years forced me to change. I have written nearly 4,000 pages journaling my journey. I have scrutinized every voice and authority from my first 40 years to redefine who I am and what I understand life to be. That includes new opinions about the Bible. As late as 37 years old, I held justifications why the Bible should be treated differently than other books. Specifically, I used to believe that it was not necessary to read the Bible in total, from the beginning. But in my desperation I questioned that premise. Then, I reasoned that if I am covered by the blood of Jesus, then I can explore the Bible on my own without consequence. I sought answers on: salvation, love, justice, identity, and peace. Those subjects sell well. In my youth I bought the counterfeit answers (lies) man sold me which kept me from going to God's Word directly. But the solitude of affliction provided a chance to remove man's weeds and plant God’s seeds in my soul. His seeds sprouted and healed the pains of death, grief, and evil. He brought me to life when I took Him at His Word. All His Words! So, if you were to ask me how I'm doing, I'd say I'm doing better than ever.

Thank you for all the ways you have loved and cared for me over the years. I wish I could reciprocate with my own love and service. Until then, I pray for eternal blessings and revelation over you and Wayne.

Love,

Dave

It is good for me that I was afflicted, that I might learn Your statutes. Psalm 119:71
